Chapter I — Playtime Slayer

“Wake the beast — 08:15 bell.”

Morning started gently in most parts of town. Not here.

Inside Willow Creek Primary, the hallways were already trembling with the nervous energy of small children and the tired resignation of the adults tasked with containing them.

Mara Quinn stood outside her classroom door, backpack slung over one shoulder, coffee cooling between her palms, watching her students gather like a slow-rolling tide. She wasn’t ready yet, not for the noise, not for the emotions, not for the swirl of contradictions that defined her days. But readiness had stopped being a requirement a long time ago. Showing up was enough.

A cluster of children stood by the lockers, giggling as they compared keychains. Another pair argued in hushed voices over who got the dinosaur sticker. A girl sat cross-legged on the floor, humming to herself as she tied and retied her shoe. Two boys chased each other in looping circles, backpacks bouncing wildly.

It looked harmless — even sweet — if you didn’t know what came next.

“Mooorning Miss Quinn!” someone shouted, already too loud for the hour.

“Good morning, Noah,” she replied, offering a smile that felt real for exactly half a second.

She glanced at the clock. 08:07.

Eight minutes to go.

The trick, she’d learned, was to observe without absorbing. To treat the pre-class chaos like weather — unpredictable, but not personal.

She sipped her too-cold coffee.

A small hand tugged her sleeve. It was Lily, her smallest student, holding up a drawing of… something that looked like a melting dragon wearing a tutu.

“Isn’t she pretty?” Lily whispered, eyes wide and hopeful.

“She’s beautiful,” Mara said softly, kneeling to meet the girl at eye level. “And very… expressive.”

Lily beamed, accepted the answer without question, and skipped away.

08:10.

The hallway noise thickened, rising in layers. Backpacks thumped. Velcro ripped. Voices bounced off the linoleum. Someone dropped a metal water bottle and it clanged like a warning shot.

Mara’s chest tightened.

She tried breathing the way her therapist once suggested — slow in, slow out — but all she tasted was the metallic tang of nerves and cheap coffee.

She opened the classroom door.

The horde surged inside.

At first, everything moved in soft motion: coats dropped onto hooks, lunchboxes stacked, chairs scraped across tile. The children spoke in excited bursts, but still in a manageable range.

They were not monsters. They were simply alive in a way adults forgot how to be. But their aliveness came at a cost.

She set her coffee down. Straightened papers. Checked the attendance sheet.

08:14.

The energy shifted. Backpacks were abandoned in the middle of the floor. Pencils became drumsticks. Someone had already disassembled a pair of scissors into two separate, highly concerning pieces.

A glitter bottle — uncapped. A paintbrush — dipped in something wet and definitely not paint. The classroom’s plastic storage bin labeled “QUIET ACTIVITIES” had been upended with ironic precision.

Mara’s pulse climbed.

08:15.

The bell rang. It didn’t sound like a bell—not to her. More like a metallic klaxon, a shriek announcing that containment was officially required, and she was the only one expected to enforce it.

They were everywhere now. On the rug. Under desks. Climbing—not metaphorically—onto shelves.

“Alright everyone, settle—”

A high-pitched screech cut her off. Something crashed behind her. Markers rolled across the floor like fleeing bullets.

The volume snapped from “busy” to unbearable without passing through any middle stage.

The room blurred into one mass of chaotic motion: two boys fought over a single orange crayon as if it were the last source of joy on Earth. A small girl sobbed because her unicorn sticker sheet had one sticker missing — the sheet she tore herself. Someone smeared glue across the whiteboard. Someone else was using a lunchbox as a drum. A third child managed, somehow, to get glitter into the ceiling fan.

The fan turned. The glitter fell like radioactive snow.

And Mara — Mara stood in the middle of it all, hands trembling, throat tight.

She felt heat crawl up her neck. A shortness of breath she tried to swallow down. Her vision pixelated around the edges.

This was not one moment. This was every moment she hadn’t processed, every day she’d held in her frustration like a breath she never got to exhale.

Her voice came out louder than she meant:

“Enough!”

It wasn’t a scream. But it was sharp. Sharper than the room deserved.

Silence hit the classroom like a hammer.

Dozens of eyes turned toward her — some confused, some scared, some simply startled. Children rarely saw adults snap. When they did, the world bent in ways they didn’t understand.

Mara’s stomach dropped. Her breath stuttered. What have I done?

Her fingers curled. Her shoulders shook. The world felt suddenly too bright, too loud, too close.

She forced the words out through a throat that felt raw.

“Recess… early. Go. Please.”

They didn’t question her. They filed out with unusual obedience, scattering into the hallway like startled birds.

The door shut behind them.

And Mara Quinn sank into her chair, heart pounding against her ribs, hands still trembling from the adrenaline.

In the distance, another class’s bell rang. Children laughed. A teacher gave instructions.

The world kept turning. Even as hers tilted.

She dropped her head into her hands.

The glitter still drifted down from the ceiling fan. Tiny flecks landing on her desk, her sleeves, her hair.

It looked like stardust.

It felt like ash.

She whispered to the empty room:

“I can’t do this anymore.”

No one answered.

Only the soft hum of fluorescent lights, flickering like a warning she should have heeded months ago.
